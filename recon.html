The Reconstrution: Citizenship

After the Civil War, America was still split into pieces. Tensions between the North and South weren't fully subdued, and racism continued. The Reconstruction supported citizenship overall, though it also introduced segregation and obstacles that hindered black people from voting; it ended slavery and allowed people of color to vote.

Abolishment

Abolishment of slavery was a necessity in supporting citizenship in the United States, putting into the 13th Amendment that no person should be treated as property. This allowed people of all color to work jobs for fair pay and to live their lives how they please.

Black Suffrage

Black men were now able to vote under the 15th Amendment, adding to their ability to voice their opinions and have a say in the US government, which is a vital part of being a citizen. With these new rights they could be able to stand up to misdoings and being able to guide the US, supporting citizenship.

Segregation

But with existing racism and an increasing racial divide, segregation of public spaces was introduced by state governments. Fear of sharing a public space with someone who was found grotesque or even unhuman caused this, leading to the restriction of citizenship, as it caused further inequality in the United States and beyond.

Blocking Colored Voting

And even yet, with black men now able to vote, new laws and groups were set into place to prohibit them from voting. The polling tax often implemented in Southern states stopped low income families, who were often black, from being able to vote. The Ku Klux Klan threatened and even killing black people and Republicans through murder and lynchings.
